About Me

My name is Violet Jessop and I’m from Bahía Blanca, Argentina. When I was a child I contracted tuberculosis, which despite doctors' predictions, I survived.

At 16 years old, my father died due to complications from surgery and my family moved to England, where I attended a convent school and cared for my youngest sister while my mother was at sea working as a stewardess.

When my mother became ill, I left school and, following in my mother's footsteps, applied to be a stewardess. Obtaining my first stewardess position with the Royal Mail Line aboard the Orinoco in 1908, I worked in the same field consistently until retirement in 1950.

Experience

RMS Olympic

In 1911 I began working as a stewardess for the White Star vessel RMS Olympic. Olympic was a luxury ship that was the largest civilian liner at the time.

In addition to this, the RMS Olympic held the status of being the lead ship of the White Star Line's trio of Olympic-class liners. The Olympic-class ocean liners included the Olympic (1911), Titanic (1912), and Britannic (1915).

I was on board on 20 September 1911, when Olympic left from Southampton and collided with the British warship HMS Hawke. Thankfully there were no fatalities, and despite considerable damage the ship was able to make it back to port without sinking.

RMS Titanic

I boarded the RMS Titanic as a stewardess on 10 April 1912, at age 24. Four days later, on 14 April, it struck an iceberg in the North Atlantic, where Titanic sank about two hours and forty minutes after the collision.

After fulfilling my duties I was ordered into lifeboat 16, and as the boat was being lowered one of Titanic's officers entrusted me a baby to look after. The next morning, the rest of the survivors and I were rescued by the RMS Carpathia.

HMHS Britannic

During the First World War, I served as a nurse with the British Red Cross. On the morning of 21 November 1916, I was on board HMHS Britannic, a White Star Liner that had been converted into a hospital ship, when it sank in the Aegean Sea after striking a deep sea mine.

Britannic sank within 55 minutes, killing 30 of the 1,066 people on board. While Britannic was sinking, myself and other passengers were nearly killed by the ship's propellers that were sucking lifeboats under the stern. In order to survive I was forced to jump out of my lifeboat, resulting in a traumatic head injury which I ALSO survived.

I returned to work for the White Star Line in 1920.

Services

Stewardess

Spending nearly 40 years look after the passengers on White Star ships, between 1910 and 1950, you'll find no one better. I am proficient in emergency protocols, knowledge of vessel safety rules and regulation, and demonstrating the use of safety equipment. As demonstrated in the past, I am able to pacify even the most irate of passengers with a courteousness and cheerful disposition.

Nurse

During the First World War, I was trained and served as a nurse with the British Red Cross on board the HMHS Britannic. Sure, 30 people died in one day once, but that was totally Captain Iceberg Charlie's fault, not mine. I did my best given a shit situation. It's a medical practice after all, not a medical perfect.

Swim Instructor

Before the HMHS Britannic sank, I didn't even know how to swim! But it turns out that being forced into the sea to avoid being turned into mince meat by the propeller of a world class ocean liner teaches you a lot. And I'm willing to impart that knowledge onto you, for a fee.

Baby Sitter

As the Titanic sunk, I was ordered into lifeboat 16. As the boat was being lowered, one of ship's officers thrust a baby into my arms. The next morning, the rest of the survivors and I were rescued by the RMS Carpathia. While on board Carpathia, the baby and thier mother were reuinted. If experience baby sitting under such dire circumstances isn't enough for you, I don't know what you want from me.
